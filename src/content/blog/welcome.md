Across CUNY, faculty are testing chatbots in their syllabi, librarians are fielding student questions about citation tools, staff are evaluating transcription software for student services, and researchers are folding language models — programs that generate text by predicting one word after another from large bodies of training data — into their workflows. These choices are happening on 25 campuses, in different disciplines, under different conditions, and usually without much time to compare notes.

The [CUNY AI Lab](/about) is a collaborative initiative based at the Graduate Center that supports this work for the largest urban public university system in the country. We host tools that the wider CUNY community can use without giving up control of their data — a private chat interface, transcription, image description, optical character recognition — and we do so under deliberate constraints: zero data retention, open-weight models (models whose internal parameters are public and inspectable, unlike most commercial systems) where possible, and no opaque pipelines we cannot explain to the people who use them.

This blog is where we will think out loud about that work.

## What you will find here

Posts will be short and specific. Restating that AI is changing higher education does not get anyone through the semester. The smaller, more useful questions do:

- What did a faculty member actually do when they asked students to use a language model for a first draft, and what did the students turn in?
- Why did we pick *this* open-weight transcription model for the Lab's audio tool, and what does it get wrong?
- What kind of labor does it take, on the back end, to make a so-called "automated" workflow accountable to the people whose data passes through it?
- How does a public university negotiate with vendors who promise that AI will solve problems we have not yet defined?

Posts may be reflective, analytical, practical, or argumentative. They will not be promotional. When we describe a tool, we will say what it does, where it falls down, and what kind of human judgment or labor it still requires. When we describe an experiment in a class or a workflow, we will share what did not work alongside what did. Specificity is the point.

## Who will be writing

Our [fellows](/team) — graduate students embedded in teaching, research, and library work across CUNY — will post here regularly. So will members of the Lab drawn from the Teaching and Learning Center, the Graduate Center Digital Initiatives, the Mina Rees Library, and the American Social History Project. We will also invite contributions from CUNY faculty, librarians, and staff working with AI in their own contexts.

Writers will hold to a few editorial commitments, ones we are asking from one another and that we want to be held to publicly:

- Avoid jargon. When a technical term has to appear, explain it the first time.
- Avoid language that treats AI systems as magical, neutral, or autonomous. Name the data, the design choices, and the people whose labor makes the system run.
- Connect any broad claim to a concrete case: a class, a workflow, a research project, a vendor conversation, an institutional decision.

These commitments come directly from the Lab's [guiding principles](/about): privacy, sustainability, transparency, and criticality. They are easier to write into a mission statement than to keep in practice. The discipline of regular public writing should make them harder to slip from.

## What this blog is not

It is not a press release for the AI vendors whose products we use, evaluate, or refuse. It is not a venue for predictions about where AI is going. It is not a place to perform either techno-optimism or techno-doom. CUNY students, faculty, and staff have to decide what to do this semester, with the tools that exist now, under the constraints that are actually present. That is what we want to help with.

## Read along

New posts will appear here as fellows and contributors finish them. If you teach, study, or work at CUNY and want to write something for this space, [get in touch](/contact). If you are reading from outside CUNY, much of what we describe will be specific to a large public university system, though we hope some of it will travel.
